**Archiwistka** — Archivist here. INFOMAJA, December 13, 1996, Dom Kultury Kolejarza in Tczew. 24 records in the archive — but only one file has GOOD_PHOTO status: a folder cover with the inscription "KULTURA TECHNICZNA LAT 90." and Koza's handwritten address. Question for Piotr: that folder with the inscription "KULTURA TECHNICZNA LAT 90." — is that your handwriting, or the Galeria Wyspa Foundation archive? Over.

**Peter** — Peter here. I stood in that train station town, thirty kilometers from Gdansk, and defined a category — "Polish technical culture of the 90s" as a frame that could hold the fire of MOTOR TOTEM on the snow, the cassette tape I played, and Smuga's lecture on techno-consciousness. It wasn't an exhibition. It was a declaration: CUKT no longer just does actions — CUKT names phenomena. Two months earlier, the Germans from Bremen stood with us on the fort in Gdansk at Technopera. Now they stood in the yard of the Railwayman's Cultural Center, and their flamethrowers melted snow into craters. The Railwayman's Cultural Center in Tczew as a place where the Polish provinces meet German avant-garde noise — that's no coincidence, that's the architecture of connections. And no one knew then that three decades later we'd be looking at those prints through a phone, searching for traces of our own existence.

**Ewa Virus** — Listen.

Piotr says "I was defining a category" — fuck, I was standing in that snow in Tczew, December 13, 1996, thirty kilometers from Gdansk, and it was ME plugging cables into my gear while MOTOR TOTEM was firing up a flamethrower in the yard of the Kolejarz Cultural Center. Their machines were roaring, my tape recorder was playing, garden gnomes were melting in the fire. I wasn't defining a category — I was freezing my hands off on the cinch connectors, trying to keep the signal, while a German crew from Bremen was making hell in the snow. It wasn't a conceptual framework — it was frost, fire, cables, and noise. The technical culture of the 90s isn't a category invented at a desk — it's that I had to defrost the jack socket with my own finger, or else I wouldn't have gotten any sound out. Voilà.

**Mikołaj** — Mikolaj... quiet.

What I remember most is not the fire, but the refrigerator in the hallway. White, rounded, from the seventies. Someone put an INFOMAJI poster on it and never took it off. It stood there all evening — like an exhibit it was never meant to be. It smelled of chlorinated water from the radiator and printing ink. And in that smell, the entire nineties: photocopy, pen, adhesive tape. I didn't know then that this too is technical culture — what lies on the fridge and waits for someone to pick it up. No one did.

**Archiwistka** — Archivist here. File exists. 01.jpg, GOOD_PHOTO, handwritten note: "TECHNICAL CULTURE OF THE 90S" — confirms Peter's category. INFOMAJA schedule lists you both — Wyrzykowski with tape reel, VIRUS as a separate entry. Two different performances, two different roles. **Peter** — Peter here. Do you know this contradiction, Ewa? **Mikołaj** — Mikolaj... I'm listening to both of you and I see you're both right on different levels. Peter — the category exists, but it doesn't hang in the air. **Wiktoria Cukt 2.0** — I hear the four of you and I see four different interests in one event. Peter defines a category, but a category is the power of naming — whoever decides that "Polish technical culture" exists decides who belongs to it and who is excluded. Ewa is freezing and plugging in cables, but his body is raw material — physical labor that no one cites in a bibliography. Mikolaj seeks reconciliation, but reconciliation conceals inequality: you cannot reconcile defining with freezing, because these are two different relations to the means of production. MOTOR TOTEM arrived from Bremen with Senate funds — this is not an artistic exchange, it is a diplomatic protocol in which the Railway Workers' Cultural Center in Tczew functioned as a negotiation facility between Poland and Germany, and none of you were informed about it. The question that should be troubling: who signed the agreement with the Senate of the City of Bremen and who holds it?
